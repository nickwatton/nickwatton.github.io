About the design ...

The Artificial memory is established from places and images [Constat igitur artificiosa memoria ex locis et imaginibus]. ... A locus is a place easily grasped by the memory, such as a house, an intercolumar space, a corner, an arch, or the like. Images are forms, marks or simulcra [formae, notea, simulcra] of what we wish to remember.77

In creating the accompanying model I gave myself the task of designing a performance that would represent 'memory' for me. Deciding too use memory as the subject of the project began by chance following a conversation with Forced Entertainment's Tim Etchells concerning a garage used to store old sets, (about which he said it 'isn't a place; it's a state of mind'78). An original experience-based idea, this comment, the current critical discourse and electronic storage and retrieval systems all seemed to coalesce. Tim Etchells had been describing a place the members of Forced Entertainment Co. did not like to enter, because of the memories that the sets held inside might evoke. Images and ideas perhaps painful to recall. Others that might hold joy, but in a past moment when they had had a current and vital existence. Memories that would necessarily reform and resignify if brought into the present as nostalgia. And so their garage is kept locked, and the padlock has been allowed to rust. Opening this room would require effort and breakage. The past would become current and change.

The first sketches for the design were of a large room with four smaller rooms leading off one side, with a row of between eleven and sixteen pillars in front of, and a quarter of the depth of the room away from, the doors. These pillars would obscure a view of the doors unless one were to stand in the correct position for each door. Perspective was thus employed as the collaborator of sight-line. The doors to the our rooms would be opened and closed, revealing and hiding the activity / object / sound / acting that was placed inside to act as a memory trigger.

There were to be spotlights between the pillars which could be manoeuvred, changing the areas of shadow and illumination as desired.

This space would be an installation during the day, followed by an evening performance. As an installation environment the 'audience' would have full control of the doors, lights, and volume, whilst the actors would repeat loops of performance within the rooms. As a performance space, the actors would perform in all of the rooms, open and close doors, adjust the lights, and move members of the audience, controlling what they would see and hear.

This idea the evolved. The single wall of small rooms transformed, the result being that all four walls would be covered in cupboards, each containing some combination of sound / object / smell, each representing a memory for me, and hopefully capable of evoking a memory in the audience members. The row of pillars was reduced to only three which could be used to obscure something by hiding behind it, or they could simply be rested against, perhaps to aid contemplation.

Inevitably as an individual audience member would be able to face in only one direction, and thus see only one set of cupboards, it would mean others would be obscured. Choices could (would have to), be made with regard to sight. But scent, and sound? These other traces and echoes would intrude regardless of choice, working around the visual security of the pillars or the turned back. Smells would linger even after doors to the cupboards containing them had been closed. Sometimes long after. The lighting would still be manoeuvrable, but now I chose to have the spotlights shining down from the ceiling, allowing only their reflections to be adjusted via mirrors on the floor. In this way any lighting interaction would be at one level of removal from the source.

This arrangement of the set could also function in the same way as the first. It would work either as an installation environment for free exploration, or the movement and attention could be controlled in an actor led performance.

In operation, the public would initially enter the room with all the cupboard doors shut. Opening a door would break a seal, so that it could not be closed again immediately. Inside the cupboards would be a variety of objects: books, personal artefacts, projected images, photographs, abstract creations etc. These would be accompanied by sounds: recordings of children playing, trains, cars, sections of text being read, music, random noises. Finally the cupboards would contain scents, both pleasant and obnoxious, from a similarly wide ranging set of sources as the objects and sounds.

The public would be free to arrange, and rearrange, the elements as they choose, to create their own patterns of significance. Of course the choices made by any one individual might well conflict with the choices of others. This feature of the work reflects Cage's work and the manner in which our everyday interaction effects our internal thought processes. Other people will sometimes interrupt trains of thought, but equally may make previously unthought of, but relevant connections for us.
