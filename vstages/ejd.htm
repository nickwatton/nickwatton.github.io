Changes in stage design through technological developments at the beginning of the Twentieth Century.

Appia, Emile Jaques-Dalcroze and Eurhythmics

Appia's work truly came to fruition through his collaboration with Emile Jaques-Dalcroze. The artistic relationship between these two practitioners was mutually beneficial, giving to Jacques Dalcroze a theatre for his Eurhythmics, and supplying Appia with a working forum for the application and development of his designs and theories of theatre production. The great excitement felt by these two visionaries on meeting a like mind is captured by Volbach in his profile of Appia, when describing his first encounter with Jaques-Dalcroze's work.

It was a revelation! Suddenly he saw the road toward a realisation of his own ideas.... When they finally exchanged ideas and combined their efforts these two artists achieved a sublime means of presenting the human body in dramatic action.17

Jaques-Dalcroze was interested in developing a form of movement which would allow an actor to make physically manifest the content of music. The resultant movement form was called Eurhythmics and described as a 'definite thought in space'.18 Where a musical score contained the visible thoughts of the composer, so the eurhythmic performer was to become the medium by which such musical expression would become physically, and thus visible for a spectator. This view of the relationship between theatre and music closely coincided with that held by Appia, allowing the works of each to complement and advance the investigations asked the other.

In response to his first encounter with eurhythmics, performed on a conventional (open) stage, Appia wrote to Jacques-Dalcroze that the dancers 'moving on a flat surface gave the impression of alpinists ascending a bas relief spread on the floor'.19 Through this, Appia described his belief that an uninterrupted stage floor lent no support to the purpose of the movement. To accompany this letter Appia created several 'Espaces Rhythmiques', spaces designed to be ideal for movement. These were composed of platforms dividing the stage into many levels, ramps, stairs, plain walls and pillars. These elements could all be moved to provide a flexible space which could be moulded to fit the needs of the drama - just as Jacques-Dalcroze's eurhythmics allowed the performer to be manipulated. In response to the set resulting from these designs Jacques-Dalcroze wrote,

For training exercises these movable elements are invaluable to me - they allow us to discover new postures in conditions of balance. But from the artistic point of view - so long as light has not come into play - the feeling of life is definitely missing and those constructions of levels and planes seem mere skeletons or dummies.20

This call for lighting struck to the core of Appia's work. It was light which unified the whole drama, enlightening performer's bodies and settings alike. Where eurhythmics was a 'definite thought in space' rather than being merely movement, so light ceased to be purely for illumination, and would 'radiate, carrying form into space'.21

The new use of light that Appia proposed was not confined to the stage but was to include moves towards the fusion of the stage and auditorium. In his design for Jacques-Dalcroze production of Orpheus and Eurydice at Hellerau (1912) Appia covered the sides of the theatre with backlit canvas walls, smoothing the transition between the bright stage hand the semi-dark house. This was part of his attempt to lessen the passivity of the audience, encouraging them to take a greater responsibility for the stage action. In 'Eurythmics and the Theatre' Appia wrote,

Up to now only quiet attention has been required of the audience. To encourage this, comfortable seats have been provided in semi-darkness, to encourage a state of total passivity - evidently the proper attitude for spectators.... Eurythmics will overturn this passivity! Musical rhythm will enter all of us, to say: you yourself are the work of art.22

The above shows a development on his earlier essay, 'Solidarity and Style' (1909), where he proposed that the artist and the public are both responsible for the creation of art. In this essay he wrote that artists should draw upon the public as the subject of their art, whilst the public must engage with the art which would thus offer up a reflection of themselves. In this way Appia's ideal audience contributed to the creation of art and were not merely consumers.23

In 'The Origin and beginnings of Eurithmics' Appia recounted an event which demonstrates how closely this view was shared by Jaques-Dalcroze. He related how in 1906 Jaques-Dalcroze held a two week course to present the results of his investigations into eurhythmics to the public.

All the participants expected that the course would be a series of lectures illustrated with student exercises, and thus edified they would return home thoroughly informed about eurhythmics. How mistaken they were! After five minutes the master [Jaques-Dalcroze] let us know that everyone had to descend into the arena to undergo the great experience for themselves. Everyone refused! Was intelligence not enough; did the whole body have to take part? Did one have to expose oneself to this ridiculous test? The moment was both comic and serious. ... It was necessary that from then on everyone should understand that eurhythmics is a personal experience and not a method. At all costs, it must be so.24

This demonstrates a vision of an equality between performer and spectator in the creation of art, shared by these two artists. This, combined with the changes in the approach to staging and movement their work represents, pointed towards a radical change in the evolution of live performance. In 'Eurythmics and Theatre' Appia warned that the new style of production should not be conducted on conventional stages. A total reworking of the system of presentation would be necessary for the vision to be realised.

Sadly, such changes were not fully realised. The work of Edward Gordon Craig which was superficially similar to Appia's, but perhaps less socially challenging,25 is well documented, (having been adapted as the heralding light of the new theatrical era). The stylistic change Craig's work represented (crossing over with Appia), provided enough of a distinction between theatre and the infant cinema to secure the position of live performance, without disrupting the social hierarchies it embodied: The passive spectator could wait patiently for Craig's Uber-Marrionette to perform for them - faultlessly, without having to use their whole body.26

The end of the century which began in this auspicious manner is beginning to realise its pedigree. The rise of the performance installation out of a cross-over between art and live performance carries with it principles of the shared responsibility between the artist and public for the creation of an art-work - principles recognisable is Appia and Jaques-Dalcroze's work. Furthermore, the insurgence of ever newer technologies, computers and the potential they offer for the creation of a new interactive performance medium, require a re-evaluation of performance at least of the magnitude Appia and Jaques-Dalcroze proposed.

Within this last century the have, of course, been practitioners and theorists who may be seen to have developed their work within areas recognisably similar to those proposed by Appia. John Cage and Merce Cunningham's work at the Black Mountain College, called for the individual to supply meaning to a performance event. Robert Wilson's experimentation with staging and light also provoked audience involvement, challenging accepted expectations about where to focus attention at any given moment of a performance. Even the traditional social centre of a theatre event - the interval - was subverted in his 'operas'. These extended performances allowed the audience freedom to enter and leave a performance as they pleased.
