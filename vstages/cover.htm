Cover Note

There was something terribly alluring about the sheet of rotting metal in the hedge. Something that meant I had to go back and check. Turning it over in my hands I was thrilled to discover the range of colours on the underside. The sheet was cold. It felt a good thickness for a cover - not flimsy, but not too thick to cut through. Carrying my chance find home, the weight tested the grip I had on it, holding it between thumb and fingers, hanging down. My thumb ached.

The qualities of texture and weight, even of temperature, could all possibly be suggested in a virtual version of the metal used for the cover of this dissertation. However, the muscular discomfort in my thumb could not begin to be evoked. Perhaps, it could be argued, the wrist-ache from using a computer mouse might suggest the carrying-ache I experienced with the metal. But no, it would be mouse-ache, not metal-ache. (It would also be a physical and not a virtual ache.)

This draws attention to the phenomenological qualities of physical as opposed to virtual objects, that cannot be transferred. This translation gap between the two environments is two-way: Just as I could not reproduce the metal-ache in the virtual environment, so the technique of morphing where (to be crude), two images are blurred together cannot be replicated in a non-virtual environment. This does not point to a lack in either. Rather, it illustrates that each environment has unique qualities, capable of complimenting and enriching the other.

For (scenographic), design this can mean that whilst a virtual model may be most suitable for representing lighting on a set, the textural quality or 'feel' of materials might be conveyed better in a physical model. In utilising new technologies people need not adopt an 'in with the new, out with the old' approach. Both physical and virtual systems of design have many real strengths and all of these should be full of advantage.

In its current state as a cover, the metal works as a bridge between the creative process of design and the practicalities of academic submission. The lock and rust (and subsequent need to break the seal to gain 'entry'), are features of the creative process, whilst the metal itself might have been an object in one of the cupboards described in the design, or indeed recall one of the cupboards themselves (see below). However, just as there are parameters in creative design there are also parameters to the form a dissertation might take. Whilst it may well have met my creative ends, to have submitted large and ragged sheets of rusted metal as part of this work may have fallen outside the bounds of academic etiquette, hence the metal is cut and contained within acetate sheets and plastic edging.
