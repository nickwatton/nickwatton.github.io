Stage Two

The influence of the New Physics on the form of performance, and the relationship with the audience, in the middle of the Twentieth Century.

Cage's collaboration with Merce Cunningham

Cage began his investigations into the performance of indeterminacy in 1952, producing Untitled Event in collaboration with Merce Cunningham at the Black Mountain College. For this Cage gave a group of dancers, actors and musicians 'time-scores', that is a list of time-intervals designating a sequence of activity or inactivity, sound or silence. The time-intervals indicated by the scores were not chosen but rather arrived at by chance, through the use of a Chinese fortune telling system. Each artist in the event was given a different score to which they were to compose - in isolation from any other artist - a performance. The event was performed without any rehearsal, and thus required that each performer fit their own composition, physical and/or aural, around those others who simultaneously occupied the space.

As a result of this structuring of the total event through completely separate elements, Cage created a performance over which no single person had total control. The performers could not completely control their individual pieces, as another artist might have needed the same space they had intended to use, forcing them to adapt and improvise. The spectator was presented with no narrative structure, indeed no single structure at all trough which they might focus on the event. Rather, they were required to be selectively attentive, concentrating now on one performer, now on another, now on the interaction between two/three/...,33 making their own connections and creating their own meanings from what they saw and heard.

As well as being an 'evening of performance that [...] created a precedent for innumerable events that were to follow',34 this production also marked a significant point in the collaboration between Cage and Cunningham. Throughout their overlapping careers they worked together in very much the manner prescribed by Untitled Event. Cunningham's dance company would prepare their performance, and Cage would compose some music. The first occasion that the two creations would come together would be at the first performance, the dancers having to (not) fit their dance to the music. The principle was one of co-existence, rather than symbiosis: any 'fit' between music and dance was let to chance, left for the audience to discover. Cage wanted people to understand by his that there is no single interpretation of events, that the individual must make their own sense of such phenomena as they discover. Paul Griffiths wrote in a review a Cage's career that 'Cage would wish that his music might be of assistance to people who must conduct themselves in a world so complex that much must seem haphazard',35

In addition to their shared compositional and performance methods, Cage and Cunningham have each also applied technologies in an innovative manner to their creative process. For example, in certain of his writings, Cage has replaced the Chinese fortune telling system with computers to randomly create the elements of his work. Cunningham, during the creation of the made-for-video dance Points in Space,36 recognised that video presented a different space to the stage. In the light of this he choreographed the piece via a camera and T.V. screen. Furthermore, the camera (and camera operator), were also choreographed ('by the end of the week [of studio rehearsals] even the camera was dancing'37).

Whilst watching the television program about Points in Space it seemed apparent that the camera choreography was ideally suited to an application of computer technology. Cunningham was forced to produce a paper score for the camera as the cost of hiring the film equipment for the rehearsal period was prohibitive. Had a (relatively), inexpensive computer system with a range of 'cameras' been used,38 then all camera movements and views could have been fully explored in advance. Due to the speed of developments in the computer graphics field, even three years ago, when Points in Space was created, such graphics systems were not available. Now that they are available, however, it would be interesting to see random generations employed to Cage to direct camera choreography. This would create a performance of music and dance, where movement, sound, and camera would all bear indeterminate relationships toward one another.
