Preface

The impulse for this dissertation resulted from a demonstration of 3D studio - new and powerful computer software which allows sophisticated computer modelling in three dimensions. Realising the potential this offered to theatre design, I chose to investigate further. It was soon apparent that before I could understand how the technological advance this represented might affect theatre design, I would first have to explore the origins of what we currently accepted as normal practice.

Two figures who revolutionised theatre design were Adolphe Appia and Edward Gordon Craig, both practising at the turn of the last century, their work simplifying, yet enriching the whole art of theatre, affecting actors as well as stages. I chose to pursue Appia rather than Craig for this project due to his position regarding audience role - requiring them to be active whilst Craig's theories left them passive (Part One, Stage 1).

This more active audience role fitted better with the interactive possibilities that current computer-influenced performance offers, and which I also picked upon in choosing to look at John Cage's influence upon performance (Part One, Stage 2). I have been deliberately selective and only addressed Cage, his collaboration with Merce Cunningham, and the work of Robert Wilson from this most fertile historical period. I could easily have examined the performance art of Yves Kline, the installations of Joseph Beuys, or the theatre of Tadeuz Kantor, but rather chose only these few examples in order to draw attention towards Cage's theories relating to the increased audience responsibility, and Appia's continuing influence seen in Wilson's use of light.

This investigative route finally led back to my starting point, the influence and use of computer technologies on current performance trends (Part One, Stage 3). Here I chose to look at the 'Hypertext' (similar in many ways to Roland Barthes' 'writerly' text), and 'Virtual Reality'. These two elements of contemporary technology and performance have radically altered the way in which works that use them are structured, performed, and 'read'.

Whilst I admit something of an historical leapfrogging, I request the indulgence of the reader in this, given the limits of time and space imposed by a Master's dissertation. I invite the reader to follow me as I make what I believe to be valid, if bold, connections between the beginning and end of the Twentieth Century. I further ask that they make connections themselves between the theory presented in Part One and the practical element, Part Two.

Part Two is comprised of a text and video element. The text describes a performance/installation, whilst the video features the design for this, produced using '3D Studio', learnt especially for this project. These work (I suggest), interactively with each other, the form of presentation intrinsically linked to the content of the design (see Part Two, Cover note). It is my intention that following Part One, Stage Three the reader will come to view part two as an 'Hypertext', and regard part one itself as an element of that hypertext, along with the video, video cover, etc...!
