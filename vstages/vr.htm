Stage Three

The impact of computer technologies on performance at the end of the Twentieth Century.

'Virtual reality', Video art, dance and performance

That virtual realty will make inroads into everyday life is beyond doubt. The television news and information services Ceefax and Teletext which only recently seemed innovative will soon be outmoded. Yet even these crude precursors of the multimedia entertainment carousels that will soon be available via our television sets are virtual spaces. Within their environments you can cal up the weather, latest news, travel reports, local cinema ... The inroads have already started to be made. Whether these tracks, their expansion into the 'information superhighway', and all that will travel down it is a good thing or not is open to debate, and some of those commenting upon it (including those I have looked at above), proceed in a contradictory manner.

The artist Hanne Tierney wrote in conclusion to her article 'Drama for Voice and Strings' last year that 'whatever television has left of our imagination is probably going to be atrophied by Virtual Reality'.62 Tierney produces an actor-less theatre in which she replaces the actors with materials, ropes, pipes etc. suspended and moved by strings. She places herself at an extreme end of what she identifies as the modernist development, citing Maurice Maeterlinck, Edward Gordon Craig, and Robert Wilson as her influences. The work explores how rhythm, line and form act in space to create meaning through the abstraction of gesture:

The gesture becomes a symbol; the imagination is permitted its own interpretations, and that in turn creates a much richer experience for the spectator.63

The underlying principle for this work is that, and here she draws a quotation from Craig's The Art of Theatre, 'there is something seemly in a man when he invents an instrument which is outside his person, and through that instrument translates his message'.64 Through this it is clear that Tierney holds that an artist always achieves her effect through tools, through that which is outside herself. She argues that even when a painter communicates suffering or joy through the human form, the expression of those emotions is achieved the skilful use of the tools: paint and brush. As she holds this aesthetic principle it seems curious that she should have such a negative view of the creative potential for the tool that virtual reality presents. Surely the potential for expression, and the creative experience, is enhanced by the introduction of a new medium. Because of this I have to disagree with the conclusion Tierney draws in her article, and its rejection of virtual reality as something which will stultify the modern spectator's imagination.

However, her argument does raise a recurring issue in writings on virtual reality which I would like to question: that the body of the artist is unsuitable as a site of experience and for creativity. The physical world of such performance artists as Lois weaver, Karen Finley or Ron Athey demonstrate that the body is most suitable as a site for the expression of creativity. In the (still small), circle of Cyber-academics65 there is a growing rejection of the body as a useful interface with virtual experience. As one of cyber-guru (and philosophy lecturer), Nick Land's students says, 'the real problem is skin, though. Skin as an organism is just very, very limited'.66 Rather than being a convincing argument that the body is limited, this reveals a restricted vision of the potential for physical stimulation. Whilst entertainment media since the early cinema have made concerted appeals to the senses, most, including virtual media, have concentrated on sight and sound. Given this preoccupation it is clear that enthusiasts of the extra-body find the body limited - yet they overlook the olfactory and tactile - the seemingly most evocative of the human senses.67

As the leading premise of this dissertation would indicate, (taking as its foundation the actor based theories of Appia rather than Craig's Uber Marionette), I am in favour of the living body as a means and site of expression. However, whilst I hold the body dear, I am also happy for its boundaries to be extended. The video artist Gary Hill seems to create just such an extension. In his installation pieces Hill combines a use of the body with the creation of a new (virtual) reality.

One of the pieces (In As It Is Always Already Taking Place), in the exhibition of his work, Tall Ships at the Liverpool Tate (April - May 1994), comprised a chest high alcove containing many differently sized video screens. Each screen displayed a different part of his body, such that his whole body was represented but dispersed, each part separate and out of sequence, to no consistent scale, no coherent orientation. The screens were piled up in clumps, an large screen showing his curved spine placed next to another bracketing off his hairy upper chest and neck, the head extended back exposing the adams-apple. Alongside was the stomach and penis, gently rising and falling with breath. His legs, starting at mid-thigh and ending above the ankles. In front of this group was another of smaller screens: thumbnail; his closed left eye and forehead - an ear. Another screen, the smallest and separate from any others, carried an ambiguous image of skin. (This last turned out to be a section from the palm of his hand.)

From outside the alcove I received a grotesque impression of the frailty of the body revealed in the severing of each part from its usual companion. The strength of a back, power of voice, delicacy and skill of hand: These associations played no part in the isolated images presented - The body limited, impotent. However, on placing my head within the alcove, I became aware of the energy, heat and sound as well as image, given off by the numerous screens. From this I developed an understanding of the separate existence (life), of each body-part/screen. Each body-part gaining identity and strength from its separation from other body-parts and its combination with technology. The breath in the stomach as no longer to fuel the (here disconnected), legs but served to gently raise and lower itself, filling and emptying the screen with steady regularity. The legs, free of their habitual burden, were beautiful in and of themselves through their positioning relative to each other, the screen, me. Man and machine (screen), had here combined to extend my knowledge of my own physicality. The virtual and the physical colluding to extend my understanding of the physical body, transforming in into a text of bliss.

As dancer Susan Kozel writes in 'Virtual Reality. Choreographing Cyberspace',

Virtual Reality is more complicated than either supreme control over the body, or simple abandonment of the body. it is a hazy realm where the body dances in and out of control.Instead of being seduced and abandoned, it seduces and abandons.68

Like the work of Gary Hill, the autobiography of Science Fiction author William Gibson can be seen to embrace these notions of control, seduction and abandonment, creating Barthes' text in which 'the subject unmakes himself, like a spider dissolving in the constructive secretions of its web'.69 In writing his autobiography Gibson enmeshed his life into a computer hypertext trough which the reader was invited to construct their own pathway. Written exclusively on computer disk the text existed only in a virtual state. As the reader read, the text destroyed itself. In this way the text took on the heat of being 'alive', incandescent for the duration through which the reader immersed themselves in the text. When the reading stopped the text ceased to exist. Can there be a more literal example of Barthes' assertion that 'meanings die when reading stops'?70

This example of a literary text makes 'hazy' the borders of performance. Constructed as a hypertext, and like a Wilson opera, each copy will be read differently, as no two readers are likely to take the path through the text. Once read (performed), the text vanishes. Like a performance the reading act Gibson's autobiography allows can only be remembered, not re-viewed. In this way Gibson has been able to open out a performance niche within the virtual environment, beyond any of Landow's visions.

Through this text / performance the reader dissolves into the (un)making of Gibson('s) 'constructive secretions': his life / text.
