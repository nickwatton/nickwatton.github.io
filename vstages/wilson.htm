Stage Two

The influence of the New Physics on the form of performance, and the relationship with the audience, in the middle of the Twentieth Century.

Robert Wilson's 'operas'

Finally in this Stage, I would like to consider the work of Robert Wilson who can be seen to have applied certain of the elements demonstrated by the Cage / Cunningham collaboration to his own work, as well as drawing upon Appia as an influence in his staging.

It is of interest to note that Wilson termed his extended duration performances 'operas', calling to mind associations with the Wagnerian influence on Appia. This reference to a musical antecedent evokes both a theatrical form organised around a music structure, and Walter Page's adage that 'all art constantly aspires to the condition of music'.39 Following the radical influence Cage exerted on the conception of music it is unsurprising that Wilson's stage productions do not directly recall Appia's. Where Appia worked within a traditional, linear and character centred, musical framework, Wilson could be said to have adopted a Cageian musical structure (non-linear, de-centred), as the foundation for his work. Despite this difference, however, Wilson's use of light to sculpt the performance space in three dimensions is clearly reminiscent of Appia's techniques. Indeed Thomas Woodruff, one of the many designers with whom Wilson collaborated has said, 'with the exception of Wieland Wagner, ... no theatre artist has taken Appia's pronouncement [that light is the primary staging device] as much to heart ... as Robert Wilson'.40

Lawrence Shyer describes the work of the lighting designer Beverly Emmons, with whom Wilson had collaborated. She was capable, at Wilson's request, of making the actors become 'luminous' on the stage, or causing them to 'appear both hot and cold at the same time'.41 Wilson used Emmons skills to focus his spectator's attention on the smallest of significant details, be that a single hand in an immense stage, or the perfect smoothness of an expanse of floor.

This precision in the use of light clearly follows Appia's example, sculpturally defining the stage through lighting. However, Wilson departed from a purist Appian approach with regard to the status of the actor. Where Appia believed that all stage elements should be used to throw the actor into relief, Wilson treated all stage elements equally, hence Emmons' lighting of both hand and floor.

Wilson's collaboration with the composer Philip Glass for the production of Einstein on the Beach followed the precedent, concerning the independence of performance elements, set by the collaboration between Cage and Cunningham.

Glass attributed the success of his collaboration with Wilson to their each giving the other 'space in which to work'.42 This space came from the relationship between music and action, where 'the music shapes the action in the same way as the visual structure does. It shares with it a full authorship role'.43 Just as in the Cage / Cunningham projects, Wilson and Glass first discussed the project and then separately composed their work. Once the compositions were complete, they were brought together as to equal layers of the final production.

In the same way as the music and visual elements were layered, so the stage images were set one behind the other, in parallel with the stage front. Each image independent from, but bearing relationships to, the others, resonance's being produced between, as well as within, the layers. Thomas Woodruff (another of Wilson's designers), describes the aesthetic and organisational principals of Wilson's stages, '[they] run in parallel lines generally and specific diagonals'.44 Accordingly Frantiäek De·k wrote in his article 'Robert Wilson' that,

Wilson's pieces are not written but designed. ... The juxtaposition of images on different horizontal planes, their focussing and defocussing, is the main principle of Wilson's theatrical assemblages.45

These assemblages of images were approached following a similar philosophy to that employed by Cage. It seems, however, that Wilson took a less systematic approach to the creation of a performance's content. His productions, whilst often being ostensibly concerned with an historic character (Einstein on the Beach, A letter for Queen Victoria, The Life and Times of Joseph Stalin), would take any association however tangentially connected with the subject given in the title. Woodruff says, 'there's this completely arbitrary way of determining the imagery. Once it's chosen everything is precisely detailed and fine-tuned but initially it comes out of nowhere.'46 & 47

This illustrates the significance given to the chance find, the empty gesture, taken and treated with the greatest significance. 'To me what something meant wasn't important', Glass said whilst working with Wilson's visual images to produce the score for Einstein on the Beach. 'What was important was that it was meaningful, that's a very important difference. ... The conviction of meaningfulness is all that was necessary for me.'48 In this way Wilson was able to construct a space for the audience to navigate, in which they could compose their own meanings. Verbal text was treated accordingly, Wilson being uninterested in the meaning of language, but rather its textural and kinaesthetic qualities, producing as a result, non-narrative verbal scores. Wilson said of this that 'we don't want to belabour you with a story'.49 The spectator therefore was to respond to the texture and energy of the language, as they did with the visual and auditory images.

Even Wilson's choice of performers contributed to this overall effect. Working mostly with amateurs, Wilson's actors were generally people performing actions, rather than characters. As such the spectators had to choose whether to view them as actors or everyday individuals. This was made more apparent as these performers were of themselves fascinatingly 'watchable', whilst as actors they were 'awkward and impersonal, inexpressive, uncommunicative ... [acting so] accordingly on stage'.50

The final element which Wilson controlled to affect audience reception of his operas was their timescale. These works were many hours long (The Life and Times of Joseph Stalin lasted 12 hours), and, as Frantiäek De·k reports, were designed to alter the perception of the audience.

The duration of [The Life and Times of Joseph Stalin] was an attempt to psychologically modify the perception of the spectator. During the twelve hours, the spectator had to accommodate himself to the slow tempo of the performance; as he become physically tired (sometimes on the verge of sleep), his mode of perception altered.51

The duration was extended partly by dramatising large stories, but more significantly by slowing down action to an exceptional degree. Simple actions like pouring a glass of water and walking across the stage with it might take half and hour. As a result all the many layers of the performance (set, lighting, actors, text, sound), could be considered separately and / or together. The audience were invited to make connections across the time and space of the performance, and its subject, in as arbitrary a manner as Wilson and his various collaborators had begun their approach to the construction of the piece.

In this way, the smallest gesture could take on huge significance as, in their somnambulant journey, the audience traversed the time and place given to them. As such reality would shift - between dream and consciousness, reality and act. Just as Cage had undermined the authority of the composer to dictate what music is composed of, so Robert Wilson had also successfully shown through his performances that what constitutes reality depends upon the perspective of the individual.

If the position suggested by the work of these practitioners is adopted, then the very notion of a singular, uniform, reality is challenged and undermined; 'reality' is exposed as a subjective position. The consequence of reality's loss of objective foundation, is that it becomes 'virtual', Since this theoretical position was 'played' out (in the 1950's ,- '70's), computer technologies have developed to a position where 'virtual realities' are very concretely in existence. The media now debates society's concerns over 'virtual-communities' where people meet and talk in an electronic space. The properties and values of the physical world are not necessarily those accepted by inhabitants of the 'virtual' communities. Certainly, on a mundane level, the solar cycle need not affect when people can 'see' each other.
