Stage 1

Changes in stage design through technological developments at the beginning of the Twentieth Century.

Adolphe Appia

Most of the major changes that overtook the theatre during the 19th Century owed more to engineers - civil, mechanical and optical - than to actors or dramatists.7

The beginning of the Twentieth Century saw a revolution in Western theatre, brought about in part by a questioning of the function of theatre, but also through a development in the use of technologies available to practitioners. The ritual function of ancient Greek theatre coupled with the introduction of Stage devices such as the eccyclema had helped to shape the staging of their dramas.8 Similarly a more egalitarian social view9 and the introduction of electric lighting (brought into theatres in the 1880's as a replacement for gas lighting10) was to significantly alter Western theatre at this time.

One figure who greatly influenced this change was Adolphe Appia. His works began with a consideration of the best manner to stage Richard Wagner's dramas and came to fruition through his collaboration with Emile Jaques-Dalcroze and the development of 'Eurythmics'.

In his book Staging a Wagnerian Drama Appia wrote that he considered contemporary staging techniques inadequate for what he saw as an entirely new theatrical form. The novelty of these dramas came, for Appia, in the totality of the vision contained in Wagner's writing. As such, Appia held that any production of Wagner's drama should be produced with the eye of an auteur. However, rather than an auteur figure - a director with an all encompassing vision or style - the individual work itself should dictate the staging. It followed from this that there could be no single approach to the Wagnerian drama (a style which Appia believed other writers would follow), could be applied to all, or any two, works.

Appia wrote,

Can these demands [made by the techniques required to Stage Wagnerian dramas] ever be laid down in rigid rules? No, because they rely on nothing but the individual drama itself, and do not rest safely on conventions, as in opera, nor on a more or less faithful replica of life, as in spoken drama. Every drama can be nothing more than a flexible tool, without rigidity determining matters.11

As this suggests, Appia saw Wagner's dramas as a move away from a naturalistic representation of events, working with a rhythm outside any pattern set by life to 'create a duration of its own'.12 This, therefore, discounted the idea of an absolute time scale for a theatre production, (challenging, for example, the convention of a three hour long evening with an interval). Rather, the correct time for a production was to be arrived at through a consideration of the intrinsic rhythm of the individual work.

The role of the actor within any staging of the work was to faithfully convey to an audience such meaning as the composer had written, without any personal interpretation. In order for this to be possible the whole scenic arrangement had to be devised so that the actor could faithfully express the content of the work. Given such an arrangement the audience could 'finally... focus entirely on the work itself: and all those worries about interpretation which at present are the death of drama, will be reduced to a point that is hardly noticeable'.13

Towards this end Appia proposed, through a series of essays and designs, a new theatre environment which would allow the actor to deliver a role with the necessary expressivity. Such a renewing of the theatre was needed as Appia saw that the proscenium arch theatre of the time was orientated around the lighting of the painted scenery and not the body of the actor. The new theatre Appia heralded hailed as its prime concern the illumination of the actor and not the set. The re-evaluation that this brought to theatre design concerned the placement of the actors physical body on stage. In his essay 'Ideas for a Reform of our Mise en Scene' he describes his ideas for the creation of staging designed around the actor where he, 'need no longer walk in front of painted lights and shadows, but instead is merged into an atmosphere which is intended for him.'14

What is crucial here is the discord Appia recognised between scenery painted in two dimensions, and the three dimensional actor. Appia realised that the bias of the theatre towards the scenic elements rather than the actor restricted the range of movement a director might wish to explore on a stage. If, for example, a backdrop was painted to create the impression of perspectives then an actor would have been limited to a shallow acting area running parallel to it in order to maintain the illusion for the audience. This forcing of the manner of staging through scenic rather than actor centred considerations would have occurred as the painted backcloth, being two-dimensional with a single perspectives view, formed a conflict with the three dimensional actor, whose size would have appeared to alter in relation to the scenery as she moved towards or away from it.

By focusing attention for his new approach on lighting, and changes in staging elements (following the replacement of scenic painting), Appia began to free the director from restrictions such as those represented by the need to maintain an artificial perspectives for the audience and opened up the whole stage area for movement. Following this simple shift in staging Appia was able to work towards the principles for production given in his essay 'Ideas for the reform of our Mise en Scene' (1902):

An object appears solid before our eyes because light strikes it, and obviously it's solidity cannot be emphasised artistically except by the artistic use of light. So much for form. The movement of the human body requires obstacles through which to express itself; all artists understand that the beauty of bodily movements depends upon various points of resistance provided by the floor and other objects.
The two fundamental conditions for an artistic rendering of the human body on stage therefore would be light that enhances its three-dimensional quality, and a solidly constructed setting that enhances it postures and movements. We are far removed from painting!15

In the years prior to this essay Appia had drawn many designs for hypothetical productions of Wagner's dramas, such as the staging for Act 3 of The Valkyrie or a forest for Parsifal (1896), it is possible to discern a development towards the use of simplified 'block' staged elements, manipulation of horizontal and vertical planes, combined with a subtle use of light. These characteristics serve to enhance the actors body whilst at the same time providing boundaries, in the form of different levels, 'walls' and areas of shadow, to restrict her movement filling Appia's 'fundamental conditions' above. Later, in the retrospective essay 'Theatrical Experiences and Personal Investigations' (1921) Appia wrote that despite 'by means of these designs I gradually achieved a purity of style which was composed for the most part by lines and forms.'16
