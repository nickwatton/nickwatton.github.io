Stage Three

The impact of computer technologies on performance at the end of the Twentieth Century.

As computers have been brought within the conscious sphere of performance, do they have brought with them new language elements, terms to describe the electronic environment: 'virtual reality', 'hypertext', 'interactive interface'. The meaning (or sense), of such descriptive terms has altered as their relationship with performance has developed. For example, an 'interactive interface' in a purely computer environment indicates a two-way communication between a computer and its (human), user: a keyboard and a screen in its simplest manifestation. Within a performance environment the communication is between audience and performer and may indicate applause, post-show discussion (formally or in the bar), or an audience's direct influence upon a production during a performance. The other two terms, above, are central to my further consideration of the effects of technologies on modern performance. I shall, for a sense of balance, approach 'hypertext' through its technological origins, and 'virtual reality' from a performance perspective.

'Hypertext' and Roland Barthes' 'writerly' text

In his book Hypertext, George Landow explores the relationship between technology and critical theory. In this work he takes into account the social environment contemporary with his writing. He characterises this as de-centred, relationships forming in 'webs' rather than progressing in a linear fashion. In taking this approach Landow is receptive to both the real experience of modern life52 and the current state of computer technology. The challenge his writing tries to address concerns what he sees as the convergence between recent theories of writing (Roland Barthes), and computer technologies which can make these textual visions apparent. For the type of text he is describing, Landow uses the term 'hypertext'.

The term hypertext was first coined by computer theorist Theodor Nelson whose definition (from Literary Machines), Landow quotes:

By hypertext I mean nonsequential writing - text that branches and allows choices to the reader, best read at an interactive screen. As popularly conceived, this is a series of text chunks connected by links which offer the reader different pathways.53

The original conception of such a reader-led literary experience was outlined by Nelson's mentor Vannevar Bush who wrote an article for Atlantic Monthly (1945) in which he described a mechanical structure called 'Memex'. This would search for and retrieve chosen texts at great speed for a reader positioned at a transparent-topped desk. Bush envisioned that not only would the reader be able to call up desired texts but that they would be able to append these texts, adding separate notes, allowing connections to be seen and noted between the text 'at hand' and others within the Memex library. In this way Bush believed that the Memex would function as 'an enlarged intimate supplement to his [the reader's] memory.'54

Making a connection between the reader led experience implied by the Bush / Nelson vision and Roland Barthes' 'writerly' text, Landow writes that 'hypertext creates an almost embarrassingly literal embodiment of ... Barthes' conception of the readerly versus the writerly text'.55

In S/Z Barthes describes the situation of the writerly text where no single text exists in isolation, but is rather an 'entrance into a network with a thousand entrances'.56 Furthermore, in Barthes' essay 'The Death of the Author' he writes of the text that it,

is not a line of words releasing a single 'theological' meaning (the 'message', of the Author-God) but a multi-dimentional space in which a variety of writings, none of them original, blend and clash.57

From this it can be seen that there is indeed a connection between the concept of hypertext and Barthes' description of the writerly text. However, Landow does not follow through the possibilities implied by the technology about which he is writing, with regard to this connection.

The exploration of this new and exciting area, the computer hypertext, seems to be an area of investigation rich in potential. Having explained Memex as the original conception of the hypertext machine, and then described Barthes' writerly text as the hypertext incarnate, Landow reduces these visionary ideas, revolutionary insights, and creative expositions into a dry and cautious singularity.

Landow has imaginatively envisaged the multiple possibilities in the work that he draws our attention to, and the convergence I mention above. However, he makes an error in seeing these two fields (critical theory and computer technology), as moving towards the same point from different directions. They are not. What he presents as 'The' convergence, is rather 'a' convergence. The two are not the same. The theories he mentions do interrelate but they are not aiming towards the same particular goal. Rather they represent theorists who herald a recognition that the organising principles of thought need not necessarily be those that Western philosophy (based upon the proposals Aristotle et. al.), has assumed them to be.

The manner in which these changes in thought are developing together is quite apparently away from the singular, towards the multiple. Landow is right to draw attention to this convergence in two seemingly disparate fields, but he does not manage to move on from the 'old' methods in his exploration of the new. What he performs is really only a slight of hand, discrediting the pre-computer textual composition by using such descriptive terms as 'old-fashioned', and 'supposedly authoritative', in order to usher in the 'new' methodology he champions to great aplomb. However, rather than actively exploring the deepest corners of the links between the new technologies and methodologies to discover their full potential, he merely reintroduces the old methods through new terminology.

Landow makes assumptions of the traditional reading pattern similar to those made by Laurel. Each assumes a linear reading (and construction), of a text, Laurel prescribing that 'drama attempts to represent ... human behaviour ... in a form that provides intellectual and emotional closure'.58 Conflicting with this, the structure of one of Wilson's 'operas' could easily be described as hypertextual, 'compose of blocks and links that permits multilinear reading paths'.59

That such performance pre-dates both Landow's and Laurel's writing is testament to a lack of foreknowledge of the field about which they have written. To demonstrate their lack of vision one need look no further than the Sunday Observer Review which ran an article covering William Forsythe's ballet 'Alie/na(c)tion'. The movement in the first section of this performance was determined by computer, resulting in a Cageian composition where, 'there was no way to keep track of the seemingly random activity, other than to pick a dancer - any dancer - and follow his or her trajectory through the maze'. The production left the reviewer Jann Parry with questions that Landow and Laurel might have done well to ask themselves: 'Why do we value harmony so much? Must we have order before we can appreciate any alternative way of looking at the world?'60

I feel that it is only fair to finally appraise Landow and Laurel's reading of their various cultural, artistic and technological texts (which have the potential to be taken as 'texts of bliss', and which they take as 'texts of pleasure'), through the words of Barthes.

Texts of pleasure: that text that contents, fills, grants euphoria; the text that comes from culture and does not break with it, is linked to a comfortable practice of reading. Text of bliss: the text that imposes a state of loss, the text that discomforts (perhaps to the point of a certain boredom), unsettles the reader's historical, cultural, psychological assumptions, the consistency of his tastes, values, memories, brings to a crisis his relation to language.61
