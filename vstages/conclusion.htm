Conclusion

It appears that those who write about computer technologies in the arts and see it as limiting, or take only a restricted view of it, bring contradictions and fears to the surface. However, where it has been taken to extend and compliment the existing human state as 'an affirmation of the fluidity of the body',71 the strengths of these developments come out. As I showed in my introduction, Western society is labouring under the carapace of a philosophy that has had more than two thousand years to harden. A result of this is a belief in a stable world view, where the twin disciplines of logic and rational thought have given untold progress. It seems reasonable, therefore, to encounter apprehension when faced with a system that develops in a non-linear manner, and along the fantastic lines of science fiction.

Artificial intelligence research runs an annual competition (the Turing test), where computer programs are tested alongside humans to see if they are approaching human intelligence levels. The 1994 Turing test of artificial intelligence did not produce a computer system that was judged to 'seem human'. However, one of the five humans in the test used to provide a measure of 'human' was judged as less human than the best program.72

The rise, and implications of artificial (non-human), intelligence has been debated for decades now, from Stanley Kubrick's 1968 film 2001: A Space Odyssey through Harry Harrison's 1993 book The Turing Option and onwards. The 1994 Turing test illustrates that 'real world' computers are still lagging behind science-fantasy visions, but still does leave me wondering about human progression (regression?) in the face of technological change. I will not fall in with the anti-computer camp (Tierney et. al.), and see this as an example of the negative influence of TV, and all that came after, upon the human race. Rather, I feel that used with ingenuity and intelligence computers are incredibly powerful tools, best exploited when people 'look the medium in its own terms, to work with the medium and find its essence'.73

Theatrical artists today are increasingly relying on computer--based tools for such tasks as lighting and scene design, lighting execution, moving scenery, designing costumes, storing and simulating dance notations, and, of course, writing scripts.74

Whilst Laurel's list above is extensive, as I have shown in the preceding Stages, it is far from comprehensive. Technologies, whether electric lighting or computers, can be (and have been), used far more creatively than Laurel indicates. As a last example of what is (will be), possible I shall refer to Ivor Benjamin, theatre writer / director and PhD student in virtual reality, who features in an Observer Review article 'Future Stages'. 'Theatre has traditionally lagged behind technology ... but there will come a point where technology can co-exist and be part of theatre.' Benjamin considers that holographic performances and performers, productions in virtual-space where 'spectators' interact with the performance, have 'got to happen by around the year 2000'. The article concludes that 'these new freedoms for theatre aren't a pipe dream; they can be achieved, if primitively, today'.75

It appears that our imaginations restrict what we will do with computer technologies (or they with us). Who knows, we may even begin to evolve together ...

I often wonder whether we'll be superseded by silicon-based life ... It's an intriguing possibility that, as we grow more and more symbiotic with our constructs, we might evolve into something interesting. ... One day the silicon side of us is going to be more comfortable than the organic side. The balance of power will shift.76
