Stage Two

The influence of the New Physics on the form of performance, and the relationship with the audience, in the middle of the Twentieth Century.

John Cage

Theatre has traditionally reflected the belief that language accurately captures reality: the principle medium of drama, as Aristotle believed it should be, has been language. We have been able to regard the text as the play itself, to analyse a play in terms of its meaning, and the speech or a character in terms of its single unified action or intention. Finding that language does not faithfully represent experience [...] the arts in general have moved away from language and have tended toward the condition of music: they have become more self-referential and untranslatable. The influence of the visual arts, moreover, has caused theatre to become more spatial, and the influence of dance has caused it to rely more on kinaesthetic expression. Because Cage empasizes music as a performance art and has conceived of it in visual and spatial terms, he, of all musicians, has most directly influenced contemporary theatre.27

The works of John Cage and those who followed or worked with him, reflect the changes in science's understanding of nature. This came about as a result of the theoretical findings of quantum physics. The central theoretical shift that quantum physics proposes is that nature is not unitary and objectively observable. Rather, the act of observing an event influences the outcome. (See note 28) If this is accepted then the perspective, both physical and mental, of an observer dictates what they see. Furthermore, no one (necessarily) subjective perspective has authority over any other, as all positions are equally subjective. This second element applies not only to people, but to anything.29 As a consequence of this, Aristotle's belief in a single underlying 'truth' or 'reality', discoverable through objective reasoning and presentable through art, has to be rejected, as there can only be subjective positions on any given subject. Moreover, Aristotle's restrictive hierarchical framework which positions Mankind centrally in the scheme of creation is also undermined. Humans are revealed to have the same (un)important status as everything else.

In aligning himself with this quantum perspective on nature, Cage had to reconsider the function of the various roles in the creation and reception of art. Given the last point, the 'artist' can have, as an example, no greater claim to the creation of music than an audience member. Equally, no sound should be raised above any other and given the distinction of being 'music'. In Actors and Onlookers Natalie Chrohn Schmitt recounts the following episode, illustrating both element of this new equality:

"But seriously", remarked one reviewer, understanding that Cage meant music to be just sounds, "if this is what music is, I could write it as well as you". And Cage agrees.30

Cage's composition 4'33" serves to illustrate how this theory functions in (musical) practice. This piece was composed for piano in three movements, where the title described the duration of the performance; 4 minutes and 33 seconds.31 Throughout the 'playing of 4'33" the pianist only touched the piano twice: Once to raise the lid at the beginning of the performance and once to lower it again at the end. Each of the three movements were to be indicated with an (undefined), hand gesture by the pianist, at again undefined intervals.

Approached from a traditional standpoint, it would appear that such a performance was silent. Nevertheless, from the Cageian perspective, the act of listening to 4'33" itself brought forth music. Each listener created their own music out of the sounds heard over the silent piano. Cage wrote of the audience that 'the hearing of the piece is his own action - that the music, so to speak, is his, rather than the composer's.'32 The performance of the score provided time and space for the contemplation of the individual's observations of the event. What 'music' any individual may have heard would depend upon their aesthetic and spatial perspective. The sounds from which an individual could compose their music would depend upon factors such as where in the room they were, the digestive noises and breathing of their neighbours. If, as mentioned above, a listener held a traditional aesthetic perspective they may well have heard no music at all.

In 4'33" the authority of the composer to dictate what their audience perceives, was clearly given up. Such a performance made explicit what was clear within less minimalist productions; it acknowledged that an artist cannot control what their audience receives from a production. Even in a traditional performance event factors like location, weather, temperament, hunger, political persuasion etc. will effect audience reception. By working in the way he did Cage deliberately utilised such commonplace indeterminacies as the strength, or in this case the focus, of his art, rather than considering them as negative distractions.
