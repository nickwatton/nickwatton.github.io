An example of this is given in 'Schrodinger's Cat':
An Opaque box is prepared for a cat to be placed inside. A random trigger device is also put into the box which will produce either food for the cat, keeping it alive, or poison, killing it; there is a 50:50 chance of either event happening. Once the box has been closed, an external observer cannot know whether the cat will live o die. Given the equal probability of either outcome, the cat can be conceived of as both dead and alive whilst the box remains closed. On opening the box the observer makes one of the possible outcomes (dead/alive) actual. Thus, if the cat is found to be dead when the box is opened, then it is the act of observation which killed it by removing the possibility that it might be alive. 'In some strange way which nobody yet understands, he [the cat] died precisely because we looked at him. Observation killed the cat.' ZOHAR, The Quantum Self. London: Flamingo, 1991, p.26.
(For a fuller explaination of this challenge to our common-sense perceptions please refer to Chapter three of ZOHAR, 'Consciousness and the Cat', pp.22-32)
