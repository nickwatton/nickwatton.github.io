Introduction

The history of Western philosophy has been irrevocably shaped (some might say overshadowed), by the methods of thought and argument laid out by the three ancient Greeks Aristotle, Socrates and Plato. The philosophies which they bequeathed to us are founded upon a notion of absolute truth, which, in its singularity, provides an irrefutable solution against which all other possible answers are wrong. This search for truth was ordered into a system of logical debate: A problem to be understood is described and the multiple positions and objections of the interested parties raised. Argument ensued, leading to a single position on the original problem, (thesis, anti-thesis, synthesis).

These theories have not just been fundamental to philosophies and political argument but also to the arts and in particular the dramatic form of theatre. These can be seen in the traditional tragic structure described by Aristotle and exemplified by Socrates: Explication; Complication; Crisis; Resolution. That this has endured for so long as a template for dramatic structure (forming the foundation of the 'well made play'), suggests the extent of Aristotle's pervasive influence and the importance of the belief structures underlying the so called 'closed endings' of conventional story telling and dramatic texts. That we have only relatively recently begun to tell stories in different ways, (fragmented narratives, ambiguous endings), suggests less a lack of creative imagination on the part of the theatre community, than the huge cultural and philosophical shifts which have followed the events of the Twentieth Century.

Whilst researching the influence of technology on theatre and the correspondence between new philosophical ideas and new types of Theatre (including work variously described as 'Live art' or 'visual theatre'), I came across various changes to the orthodoxy of Aristotle et al. However, first I would like to briefly consider the recent and surprising recycling of Aristotle theories in conjunction with computer technologies.

Brenda Laurel, interface designer for Apple computers and author of Computers as Theatre, writes in response to being questioned about why the Aristotelian dramatic structure has been so enduring that,

without doubt there are more recent theorists who have made major contributions to the body of dramatic criticism. But no-one has provided a theory of the drama that is as comprehensive and well integrated as Aristotle's.1

In her role as a designer for Apple computers, Laurel is interested in providing a 'user-friendly' interface between the computer and human user. Such a meeting point must provide both the computer and the human with a method of giving and receiving information which functions well for each of them. It is Laurel's belief that drama provides a perfect model for such an exchange, as a method of debate and also functioning as a forum for the communication of ideas. She describes Aristotle's theory as an ideal template for what she terms 'human-computer activity' in the following manner:

A reason for looking at the Poetics as opposed to more contemporary theories (such as post-modernism) is that the Aristotelian paradigm is more appropriate to the state of the technology to which we are trying to apply. In order to build representations that have theatrical qualities in computer-based environments, a deep, robust, and logically coherent version of structural elements and dynamics is required - and this is what Aristotle provides.2

This perception of Aristotle's Poetics as a coherent foundation for human-computer relations is however based upon a narrow definition of theatre. One which 'attempts to represent ... human behaviour ... in a form that provides intellectual and emotional closure'.3 It is peculiar that Laurel should adopt a linear and closed theatrical model for her work with computers, which elsewhere she describes as being non-linear, and open ended.4 This represents a mismatch in terms of the deep, underlying structures of the two sides of the relationship.

It seems particularly narrow-minded to tie the latest technology within the confines of ancient classical thought. This suggests a worrying tendency to restrict the potential of new forms within the safety of tried and tested structures, rather than exploring the possibilities beyond present limits (a theme to which I will return later). The declaration of her awareness of post-modernism makes her position even more untenable. Within a post-modern framework it would be possible for example, to draw analyses between the theories of Roland Barthes, computer, and modern performance. Barthes' theories (especially that of the 'writerly text'), might be used in such an analogy, as whilst non- linear, they are both rigorous and coherent.5

In order to re-evaluate live performance and scenic design as a complete event rather than from the traditional basis of textual criticism, I have sought out critical thinking which challenges the restrictions of Aristotelian thought. As shown briefly below these include cultural commentators as well as philosophers, in order to attempt a consideration of a range of thought which connects with the writings/practices of Appia, Cage and Wilson. This is important because I seek to integrate theory and practice and the introduction of texts from different disciplines.

Edward de Bono, in his book Parallel Thinking, attempts to undermine, overturn and think beyond the Socratic methods of Western philosophy in order to fracture what he sees as the 'straight-jacket'6 of traditional linear thought. For example, de Bono suggests that we might approach everyday problems by writing down all the possible solutions without value judgement (in parallel) before creatively evaluating each of the possibilities. This somewhat egalitarian method attempts to avoid the reactive binary procedures of problem/answer by refusing to solve the problem until the unlikeliest of possibilities has been mined for any hidden potential.

Unfortunately de Bono's text becomes circumscribed by the inescapably commercial nature of its intended use. Eventually, he, like Laurel is undermined by the reality of his own methodologies, and although he attempts to think himself outside the 'box' of traditional thinking he is very far from the radical philosophical provocation's of Roland Barthes - the form of whose writing embodies its contents.

Leaving de Bono's theoretical excursion aside, I would like to consider the recent use of technological innovation in performance (including computer generated performance), through the perspective offered by the work of Adolphe Appia, John Cage and his use of quantum theory and finally the 'operatic' events of Robert Wilson.
